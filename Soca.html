Music, Politics, and Pleasure

Exploring Jocelyne Guilbault’s analysis of live soca music in Trinidad, focusing on public intimacy, pleasure, identity, and politics.

Overview

Jocelyne Guilbault’s article “Music, Politics, and Pleasure: Live Soca in Trinidad” examines how soca music performances create powerful social experiences through pleasure, movement, and collective participation.

Rather than viewing politics only as government commentary or protest, Guilbault argues that soca performances are political because they shape identity, relationships, belonging, and public interaction.

“Pleasure is indeed not just central to soca music making. It is a vital force in soca community formation and consumer loyalty.”

Artists & Collaboration

Since the 1990s, soca artists have increasingly collaborated across ethnicity, nationality, gender, and musical genre. These collaborations challenge older calypso traditions that focused mainly on solo performers.

Cultural Mixing

Soca blends influences from dancehall, hip-hop, pop, and Caribbean musical traditions.

Shared Performance

Artists perform together on stage, creating new musical relationships and experiences.

Identity

Collaboration reflects changing Trinidadian identity and globalization.

Examples include collaborations between Trinidadian and Jamaican artists, Indo- and Afro-Trinidadian performers, and musicians from different Caribbean islands.

Audience & Participation

Live soca performances rely heavily on audience participation. Through call-and-response singing, dancing, waving flags, and synchronized movement, crowds become active participants rather than passive listeners.

“The focus of live performances is interaction—sensual, emotional, and physical.”

Call & Response

Artists encourage audiences to sing, jump, wave, and move together.

Community

Shared movement creates a strong feeling of collective belonging.

Posses

Friends attend events together for safety, enjoyment, and social bonding.

Guilbault explains that these events create “public intimacies,” where strangers interact closely through music, dance, and shared emotion.

Politics & Pleasure

The article challenges the idea that political music must contain direct protest lyrics. Soca performances are political because they influence social values, identity, and public behavior.

Inclusion

Soca can unite people across race, nationality, and class.

Exclusion

Certain groups and identities remain marginalized, including LGBTQ+ expression.

Class Differences

Expensive ticket prices and VIP sections create unequal access to performances.

The article also discusses gender expectations, tourism, public safety, and the commercialization of carnival culture.

Conclusion

Guilbault concludes that pleasure itself is politically meaningful. Soca performances create spaces where people form relationships, express identity, and experience freedom through music and dance.

“Rather than endorsing the normative opposition between pleasure and politics, this study underscores the productive political force of pleasure in soca performances.”

Through collective participation and emotional connection, live soca performances become powerful cultural and political experiences.
